Improving the Air Inside Your Home
Most people spend 90% of their time indoors — and indoor air can actually be 2 to 5 times more polluted than outdoor air. The good news: there are simple things every homeowner can do to make a real difference.
🔄
Change Your Air Filter Regularly
Your HVAC filter is your home's first line of defense. A standard 1-inch filter should be replaced every 30–60 days. Look for a MERV-8 or higher rating — the higher the number, the smaller the particles it catches. A clogged filter doesn't just hurt air quality, it makes your system work harder.
🌬️
Ventilate Smartly
Open windows when outdoor AQI is Good or Moderate (below 100) — especially in the morning before heat builds. On bad air days, keep things sealed up. Running your bathroom and kitchen fans while cooking or showering removes moisture, odors, and combustion gases before they build up.
💧
Control Indoor Humidity
Austin summers are humid. Keeping indoor humidity between 30–50% is the sweet spot — low enough to discourage mold and dust mites, but not so dry that it irritates your skin and airways. A dehumidifier in problem areas makes a noticeable difference in how the air feels and smells.
🚫
Reduce Indoor Pollutant Sources
Common indoor air offenders include scented candles, aerosol sprays, gas stoves, and synthetic air fresheners. These release VOCs (volatile organic compounds) into the air you breathe. Swap to beeswax candles, use the range hood when cooking gas, and choose fragrance-free cleaning products.
🌿
Add a Few Houseplants
Plants like peace lilies, snake plants, and spider plants can absorb small amounts of common indoor pollutants. They're not a replacement for filtration, but they add oxygen, reduce stress, and make your home feel more alive. Bonus: they act as natural humidity indicators — droopy leaves mean dry air.
🐾
Pet Owners: Extra Steps Matter
Pet dander is one of the most common indoor allergy triggers. Vacuum at least twice a week with a HEPA-filter vacuum, wash pet bedding weekly, and keep pets off furniture if allergies are a concern. On high pollen days, wipe your pet's paws and coat when they come inside — they track allergens in.
