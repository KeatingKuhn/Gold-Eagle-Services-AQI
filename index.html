Austin Allergy Calendar
Austin is one of the top 5 worst allergy cities in America — and unlike most places, something is almost always in bloom. This calendar shows the typical ebb and flow of Austin's allergens month by month so you can plan ahead.

🌲 Cedar Fever Season (December – February) — Mountain Cedar (Ashe Juniper) is Austin's most notorious allergen. During peak release, the pollen cloud can be so thick it looks like smoke rolling through the Hill Country. Symptoms include runny nose, itchy eyes, fatigue, and sometimes a low-grade fever — so intense that newcomers often mistake it for the flu. If you're new to Austin, your first Cedar season is typically the worst, as your immune system has had zero prior exposure.

Improving the Air Inside Your Home
Most people spend 90% of their time indoors — and indoor air can be 2 to 5 times more polluted than outdoor air. Small changes make a real difference.
🔄
Change Your Air Filter Regularly
Your HVAC filter is your home's first line of defense. Swap it every 30–60 days and look for MERV-8 or higher. A clogged filter hurts air quality and makes your system work harder and cost more to run.
🌬️
Ventilate Smartly
Open windows when outdoor AQI is Good or Moderate (below 100), ideally in the morning before heat builds. On high-pollen or bad-air days, keep things sealed up. Kitchen and bathroom fans remove moisture and gases before they build up inside.
💧
Keep Humidity in Check
Austin summers are humid. The sweet spot for indoor humidity is 40–55% — low enough to discourage mold and dust mites, but comfortable for your airways and skin. A dehumidifier in problem areas makes a noticeable difference.
🚫
Cut Back on Indoor Pollutants
Common offenders: scented candles, aerosol sprays, gas stoves, and synthetic air fresheners. These release VOCs into the air you breathe. Use the range hood when cooking, choose fragrance-free cleaners, and swap to beeswax or soy candles.
🌿
Add a Few Houseplants
Plants like peace lilies, snake plants, and spider plants absorb small amounts of common indoor pollutants. They're not a replacement for a good filter, but they help — and droopy leaves usually mean the air is too dry.
🐾
Pet Owners: A Few Extra Steps
Pet dander is one of the most common indoor allergy triggers. Vacuum twice a week with a HEPA-filter vacuum, wash pet bedding weekly, and on high-pollen days — wipe your pet's paws and coat when they come inside. They track allergens in every time.
