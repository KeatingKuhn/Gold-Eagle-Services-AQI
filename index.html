🌲 Cedar Fever Season (December – February) — Mountain Cedar (Ashe Juniper) is Austin's most notorious allergen. During peak release, the pollen can be so heavy it looks like smoke rolling through the Hill Country. Symptoms include runny nose, itchy eyes, fatigue, and sometimes low-grade fever — leading many people to mistake it for the flu. If you're new to Austin, your first Cedar season is often the worst as your immune system has no prior exposure.
Improving the Air Inside Your Home
Most people spend 90% of their time indoors — and indoor air can actually be 2 to 5 times more polluted than the air outside. The good news: small changes make a real difference.
🔄
Change Your Air Filter Regularly
Your HVAC filter is your home's first line of defense. A standard 1-inch filter should be swapped out every 30–60 days. Look for a MERV-8 or higher rating — the higher the number, the smaller the particles it catches. A clogged filter hurts air quality and makes your system work harder.
🌬️
Ventilate Smartly
Open windows when outdoor AQI is Good or Moderate (below 100), ideally in the morning before the heat builds. On bad air or high-pollen days, keep things sealed up. Running bathroom and kitchen fans removes moisture, odors, and combustion gases before they build up inside.
💧
Keep Humidity in Check
Austin summers are humid. The sweet spot for indoor humidity is 30–50% — low enough to discourage mold and dust mites, but not so dry that it irritates your airways. A dehumidifier in problem areas makes a noticeable difference in how the air feels and smells.
🚫
Cut Back on Indoor Pollutants
Common indoor air offenders: scented candles, aerosol sprays, gas stoves, and synthetic air fresheners. These release VOCs into the air you breathe. Use the range hood when cooking, swap to fragrance-free cleaning products, and choose beeswax or soy candles if you like the ambiance.
🌿
Add a Few Houseplants
Plants like peace lilies, snake plants, and spider plants absorb small amounts of common indoor pollutants and add oxygen. They're not a replacement for a good filter, but they help — and act as natural humidity indicators. Droopy leaves usually mean the air is too dry.
🐾
Pet Owners: A Few Extra Steps
Pet dander is one of the most common indoor allergy triggers. Vacuum at least twice a week with a HEPA-filter vacuum, wash pet bedding weekly, and on high pollen days — wipe your pet's paws and coat when they come inside. They track allergens in every time.
