Austin Allergy Calendar

🌲 Cedar Fever Season (December – February) — Mountain Cedar (Ashe Juniper) is Austin's most notorious allergen. During peak release, the pollen cloud can be so thick it looks like smoke rolling through the Hill Country. Symptoms include runny nose, itchy eyes, fatigue, and sometimes a low-grade fever — so intense that newcomers often mistake it for the flu.

Improving the Air Inside Your Home
Most people spend 90% of their time indoors — and indoor air can be 2 to 5 times more polluted than outdoor air. Small changes make a real difference.
🔄
Change Your Air Filter Regularly
Your HVAC filter is your home's first line of defense. Swap it every 30–60 days and look for MERV-8 or higher. A clogged filter hurts air quality and makes your system work harder.
🌬️
Ventilate Smartly
Open windows when outdoor AQI is Good or Moderate (below 100), ideally in the morning. On high-pollen or bad-air days, keep things sealed up and run your air system.
💧
Keep Humidity in Check
The sweet spot for indoor humidity is 40–55% — low enough to discourage mold and dust mites. Austin summers regularly push above 60%. A whole-home dehumidifier makes a noticeable difference.
🍄
Control Mold at the Source
Mold needs moisture. Fix any leaks, run bathroom fans after showers, and keep indoor humidity below 55%. An ERV (Energy Recovery Ventilator) brings in fresh air without bringing in humidity.
🚫
Cut Back on Indoor Pollutants
Common offenders: scented candles, aerosol sprays, gas stoves, synthetic air fresheners. These release VOCs. Use your range hood when cooking and choose fragrance-free cleaners.
🐾
Pet Owners: A Few Extra Steps
Pet dander is one of the most common indoor allergy triggers. Vacuum twice a week with a HEPA-filter vacuum, and on high-pollen days wipe your pet's paws and coat when they come inside.
